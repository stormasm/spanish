Spanish possessive adjectives are divided into short-form (placed before the noun) and long-form (placed after the noun or used with the verb ser).  The forms for "our" (nuestro) and "your" (vuestro) are unique because they must agree in gender and number with the object possessed, whereas mi, tu, and su only change for number.

Short-Form Possessive Adjectives (Before the Noun)
These are the most common forms and do not use articles (e.g., mi libro, not la mi libro).

- Placement: Short forms precede the noun (e.g., nuestra casa), while long forms follow it (e.g., la casa nuestra) or stand alone with an article (e.g., la nuestra).
- Agreement: Unlike English, Spanish possessives must match the gender and number of the object owned, not the owner.  For example, nuestras maletas (our suitcases) is feminine plural because maletas is feminine plural.
- Clarification: Because su/sus can mean "his," "her," "your," or "their," speakers often add clarification like su coche de él (his car) if the meaning is unclear.
