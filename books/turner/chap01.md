

What is Mexico? North Americans commonly call Mexico "our sister republic."

Most of us vaguely describe it as a republic similar to our own, inhabited by people a little different in temperament, a little poorer, and a little less advanced, but enjoying the protection of republican laws: a free people in the sense that we are free.

Some of us who have seen the country through a train window, or observed it in the mines or on the haciendas, describe this land south of the Rio Grande as governed by a benevolent paternalism, in which a great and good man arranges everything well for his foolish but adored people.

I found that Mexico was none of those things. I discovered that the real Mexico is a country with a Constitution and written laws as generally just and democratic as ours; but where neither the Constitution nor the laws are enforced. Mexico is a country without political freedom, without freedom of speech, without a free press, without free elections, without a judicial system, without political parties, without any of our cherished individual rights, without the freedom to pursue happiness. It is a land where for more than a generation there has been no electoral contest for the Presidency; where the Executive Branch governs everything through a standing army; where political positions are sold at a fixed price. I found that Mexico is a land where people are poor because they have no rights; where peonage is commonplace for the masses and where effective slavery exists for hundreds of thousands of men. Finally, I found that the people do not adore their President: that the mark of the opposition, until now contained and kept at bay by the army and the secret police, will soon breach this wall. Mexicans of all classes and affiliations are in agreement that their country is on the verge of a revolution in favor of democracy: if not a revolution during Díaz's time, since he is already old and expected to die soon, then a revolution after Díaz.

My special interest in Mexican politics was first sparked in early 1908, when I made contact with four Mexican revolutionaries who were then imprisoned in the Los Angeles, California, municipal jail. They were four educated, intelligent, university-educated Mexicans, detained by the United States authorities on charges of planning the invasion of a friendly nation, Mexico, with an armed force from U.S. territory.

Why would cultured men want to take up arms against a republic? Why did they need to come to the United States to prepare their military maneuvers? I spoke with these Mexican detainees. They assured me that for some time they had been peacefully agitating
in their own country to overthrow, without violence and within the constitutional framework, the people
who controlled the government.

But for this very reason, they declared, they had been imprisoned and their property destroyed. The
secret police had been tracking them, their lives threatened, and
every kind of method had been employed to prevent them from continuing their work. Finally, pursued as
criminals beyond national borders, deprived of the rights of freedom of
speech, of the press, and of assembly, deprived of the right to organize peacefully to
promote political change, they had resorted to the only alternative: arms. Why
did they wish to overthrow their government? Because it had disregarded the Constitution; because
it had abolished the civil rights that, according to the consensus of all enlightened men. They are
necessary for the development of a nation: because he had dispossessed the people of their lands:
because he had turned poor workers into serfs, laborers, and some of them even into veritable slaves.

“'Slavery'? Do you want me to believe that there is still real slavery in the Western Hemisphere?” I replied mockingly. “Bah! You talk like any American socialist. You mean 'wage slavery,' or the slavery of miserable living conditions. You don't mean human slavery.”

But those four exiled Mexicans insisted:

“Yes, slavery,” they said, “real human slavery. Men and boys bought and sold like mules, exactly like mules, and as such belong to their masters: they are slaves.”

“Human beings bought and sold like mules in America? In the 20th century! Well,” I told myself, “if this is true, I have to see it.”

And so it was that, in early September 1908, I crossed the Rio Grande on my first trip, passing through the checkpoints of Old Mexico. On this, my first trip, I was accompanied by L. Gutiérrez de Lara, a Mexican from a distinguished family, whom I also met in Los Angeles. De Lara opposed the existing government in Mexico, a fact that my critics have pointed to as evidence of bias in my research. Each of the fundamental facts mentioned regarding slavery in Mexico I saw with my own eyes or heard with my own ears, and almost always from the lips of people perhaps inclined to downplay their own cruelties: the slave overseers themselves. The coast of Yucatán, which comprises the north-central part of the peninsula, is located almost

The secret of these peculiar conditions lies in the fact that the soil and climate of northern Yucatán are perfectly suited to the cultivation of those hardy, centuries-old plant species that produce henequen, or sisal fiber. There lies Mérida, a beautiful, modern city with a population of 60,000, surrounded and sustained by vast henequen plantations, where rows of gigantic green agave plants stretch for many kilometers. The haciendas are so large that each one has its own small town, with 500 to 2,500 inhabitants depending on the size of the estate, and the owners of these large tracts are the main slave owners, since the inhabitants of these towns are all slaves. The annual export of henequen is approximately 113,250 tons. The state's population is around 300,000, of whom 250,000 form the group of slave owners. But the largest area and the majority of the slaves are concentrated in the hands of 50 henequen kings. There are more than 100,000 slaves.

With the aim of learning the truth from the slaveholders themselves, I mingled with them,
concealing my intentions. Long before setting foot on the white sands of Progreso, the port of
Yucatán, I already knew how visiting researchers were bought or deceived; and if they
could not be bribed, they were invited to eat and drink until they were stuffed, and once thus
flattered, their heads were filled with falsehoods and they were led along a pre-arranged route. In short: they were so completely deceived that they left Yucatán with the
half-believed belief that the slaves were not slaves at all; that the 100,000 hungry, exhausted, and
degraded laborers were perfectly happy and so content with their lot that it would be
a true disgrace to grant them the freedom and security that rightfully belong to
every human being.

The role I played in the charade in Yucatán was that of a wealthy investor
wanting to invest in henequen plantations. As such, the henequen barons
received me warmly. I was truly fortunate to arrive in the state at that time, because
before the panic of 1907, it was a well-understood policy, unanimously approved by the Agricultural Chamber, the farmers' organization, that foreigners should not be allowed to know about the henequen business. This stance stemmed from the enormous profits and the wealthy
Yucatecans wanting to keep all the power for themselves; but, especially, from the fear that
foreigners would expose all their misdeeds to the world.

The panic of 1907 ruined the henequen market for some time. The henequen growers were a
group of small-time Rockefellers, but they needed cash and were willing
to accept it from the first person who came along. Therefore, my imaginary capital was the "open sesame" to
enter their group and their estates. I not only discussed each phase of
henequen production with the kings themselves, but while I was free from their watch, I observed the
normal living conditions of thousands of slaves.
The most prominent among the henequen kings of Yucatán is Olegario Molina, former governor of the
state and Secretary of Development of Mexico. His properties, both in Yucatán and
Quintana Roo, cover more than 6 million hectares: a small kingdom.

The 50 henequen kings live in opulent palaces in Mérida, and many of them have homes
abroad. They travel extensively, speak several languages, and with their families constitute a highly cultured
social class. All of Mérida and all of Yucatán, and even the entire peninsula, depend on these
50 henequen kings. Naturally, they dominate the politics of their state and do so for their own benefit. The slaves are: 8,000 Yaqui Indians, imported from Sonora; 3,000 Chinese (Koreans); and between 100,000 and 125,000 Mayan indigenous people, who previously owned the lands now dominated by the henequen barons. The Mayan people likely represent almost 50% of the Yucatecan population, and even the majority of the 50 henequen kings are of mixed Mayan and Spanish ancestry. The Maya are indigenous, though not "Indians" in the common North American sense of the word. They are not like those in the United States and are called that only because they inhabited the Western Hemisphere when the Europeans arrived. The Maya had their own civilization when the Spanish "discovered" them, and it is known that their civilization was as advanced as that of the Aztecs of central Mexico or the Incas of Peru. The Maya are a unique people. They are unlike any other people in the world; not like other Mexicans, nor like North Americans, nor like the Chinese, nor like Hindus, nor like Turks.

But one can imagine that the fusion of these five very different peoples could form a people like the Maya. They are not tall, but their features are delicate, and their bodies give a strong impression of grace and elegance: olive skin, high foreheads, and slightly aquiline faces. In Mérida, women of all classes wear wide, waistless white dresses, embroidered along the hem and around the neckline with bright colors: green, blue, and red. During the always warm evenings, a military band plays music, and hundreds of graceful women and girls, dressed in this attractive way, mingle among the fragrant flowers, artistic statues, and tropical greenery of the main square.

How are the slaves recruited? Don Joaquín Peón informed me that Mayan slaves

The amount of the debt doesn't matter; the main thing is that it exists, and the small operation is
carried out by people who combine the roles of loan sharks and slave drivers.
Some of them have offices in Mérida and get free workers, employees, and

Thus, the debt system in Yucatán not only fails to alleviate the slave's plight but actually makes it harsher. It increases its severity because, besides not helping him escape the abyss, its tentacles also ensnare his brother. The portion of the Yucatecan population born free does not possess the "inalienable right" to their liberty. They are free only on the condition of becoming prosperous, but if a family, no matter how virtuous, dignified, or cultured, falls into the misfortune of its parents incurring a debt and being unable to repay it, the entire family is at risk of falling under the control of a henequen plantation owner. Through debt, the slaves who die are replaced by the unfortunate wage laborers of the cities.
Why do the henequen barons call their system forced labor for debt, instead of calling it by its true name? Probably for two reasons: because the system is
a derivative of other, less rigid systems that were essentially debt bondage; and because of
the prejudice against the word slavery, both among Mexicans and foreigners.
Debt bondage, in a more modern form than in Yucatán, exists throughout Mexico and is called
peonage. Under this system, the police authorities everywhere recognize the right
of a landowner to physically seize a worker who is indebted to him and
force him to work until the debt is paid. Naturally, once the employer can
force the worker to work, he can also impose the conditions of the work, which
means that these conditions will never allow the debtor to be freed from his debt.

Such is peonage as it exists throughout Mexico. Ultimately, it is slavery; but the
landowners control the police, and the supposed distinction is maintained nonetheless. Slavery is peonage taken to its extreme, and the reason it exists in Yucatán lies in the fact that, while in some other areas of Mexico a portion of the dominant interests oppose peonage and, consequently, exert a certain influence that modifies it in practice, in Yucatán all those who control the situation are dedicated to the exploitation of henequen, and the cheaper the worker, the greater the profits for everyone. Thus, the peon becomes a slave. The henequen barons try to excuse their system of slavery by calling it forced labor for debt.

"Slavery is against the law," they say. "It is against the Constitution."

When something is abolished by the Constitution, it can be practiced with fewer obstacles if it is given another name; but the fact is that debt service is as unconstitutional in Mexico as slavery. The claim of the henequen barons to remain within the law
is unfounded. A comparison of the following two articles of the Mexican Constitution
proves that the two systems are considered equal:
"Art. I, Section 1. In the Republic, all are born free. Slaves who enter the national territory regain their freedom by that very fact and have the right to the protection of the laws."

"Art. Y, Section 1 (amended). No one shall be compelled to perform personal labor without just remuneration and without their full consent. The State shall not permit the fulfillment of any contract, agreement, or accord that has as its object the reduction, loss, or irreversible sacrifice of personal liberty, whether for reasons of work, education, or religious vows. No pact shall be tolerated in which an individual agrees to their proscription or exile."

Thus, the slave trade in Yucatán, whatever it may be called. It always
turns out to be unconstitutional. On the other hand, if the current government's policy is to be taken as law,
the business of slavery in Mexico is legal. In that sense, the henequen barons
"obey the law."

"It's necessary to beat them; yes, very necessary," he told me with a smile,

One of the first scenes we witnessed on a henequen plantation was that of a slave being whipped: a formal beating in front of all the laborers gathered after roll call early in the morning. The slave was tied to the back of a huge man and given 15 lashes on his bare back with a thick, wet rope, with such force that blood ran down the victim's skin. This method of whipping is very old in Yucatán and it is customary on all plantations to apply it to young men and adults alike, except for the largest men. Women are forced to kneel to be whipped, and the same is often done to heavy men. Both men and women are beaten, either in the fields or during roll call in the mornings. Each overseer carries a heavy stick with which he pokes, harasses, and beats the slaves at will. I don't recall ever visiting a single henequen plantation where I didn't witness this practice of prodding, harassing, and beating people continuously.

I saw no punishments worse than whipping in Yucatán, but I heard about them. I was told of men who had been hung by their fingers or toes to be flogged; of others who were locked in dark, dungeon-like chambers, or had drops of water poured onto their palms until they screamed. In extreme cases, the punishment for women consisted of violating their modesty. I saw the dark dungeons, and everywhere I saw the prison-dormitory areas, the armed guards, and the night watchmen who patrolled the plantation grounds while the slaves slept. About 700 slaves were men fit for work, and the rest were women and children; 380 of them

beans, salt, chili peppers, blankets, and throws were all that was available—and I noticed that these prices were
high. I don't understand how a family could live on the goods they were given
worth 25 cents a day, especially considering they work so hard.

The slaves get up when the large bell in the courtyard rings at 3:45 in the morning, and
their work begins as soon as they can reach the fields. Work in the fields ends
when it gets too dark to see, and in the "house" it sometimes continues for many
hours into the night.

The main work on the hacienda consists of cutting henequen leaves and clearing the land
of weeds that grow among the plants. Each slave is assigned a task
of cutting a certain number of leaves or clearing plants, and the landowner tends to
set quotas so high that the slave is forced to call his wife and children to help him; in this way, almost all the women and children on the hacienda spend part of the
day in the fields. Single women are in the fields all day, and when a
boy reaches twelve years old, he is considered a working man and is given a quota
that he must fulfill on his own. On Sundays, the laborers do not work for their master; they spend
the time in their gardens, resting, or visiting each other. Sundays are the days when the young men and women meet and make plans to marry. Sometimes the farmhands are allowed to leave the estate to visit the neighbor's slaves; but they are never allowed to marry people from other estates, because that would mean one or the other of the landowners would have to buy the wife or husband, which would create difficulties.

Such are the conditions that generally prevail on all henequen plantations in Yucatán.

We spent two days at the hacienda called San Antonio Yaxché and became intimately familiar with its work system and its people. The owners of the large plantations do not sleep there, nor do the administrators; like the owners, the administrators have their homes and offices in Mérida and visit the plantations only two to six times a month. The head steward is usually the supreme authority on the plantation; but when the administrator arrives, the head steward becomes a truly insignificant figure.

At least that was the case at San Antonio Yaxché. The steward was obliged to bow and pay homage to his superior, just like the lesser overseers; And at lunchtime, Manuel Ríos, the administrator, my companion—much to Ríos's chagrin, as he saw him as a subordinate—and I ate alone with great ceremony, while the steward circled the table, ready to rush out and fulfill our every request.

During our first meal, which was the best I tasted in all of Mexico, I felt a strong urge to invite the steward to sit and have something to eat; but I didn't, and later I was glad, because before leaving the hacienda I realized I had committed a terrible mistake.

In the fields we saw groups of men and boys, some clearing the weeds that grow among the gigantic plants and others cutting the enormous agave leaves with machetes.
These leaves are harvested continuously throughout the twelve months of the year, and during this period each plant is inspected four times. Twelve leaves are usually cut, the largest ones,
leaving the thirty smaller ones to grow for three months. The worker cuts the leaf
at its root: he removes the thorns from the edges; he removes the terminal spine; he counts the leaves that
remain on the plant and those that have been cut, he stacks them into bundles and, finally, he takes

Once cut, the henequen leaves are taken to a large building constructed in the middle of the main hacienda, where they are lifted by forklifts and placed on a conveyor belt that takes them to the shredder. This is a machine with strong steel teeth that scrape the thick leaves, resulting in two products: a green powder, which is waste, and long, hair-like, greenish fibers, which is henequen. The fiber is then taken by streetcar to the drying shed, where it acquires the color of the sun. It is then transported by streetcar, pressed into bales, and a few days or weeks later, the observer can see it in Progreso, the port of Yucatán, about 35 km north of Mérida, where it is loaded onto a ship, usually British. The United States buys almost all the henequen from Yucatán, of which our rope-making trust, considered an affiliate of Standard Oil, absorbs more than half. In 1908, the price of henequen fiber in bales was eight cents per pound, and a slave trader told me that its cost of production was no more than one cent. More than 300 slaves sleep in a large stone and mortar structure, surrounded by a solid four-meter-high wall, topped with shards of glass. This enclosure is accessed through a single door, guarded by a man armed with a club, saber, and pistol.

This was the dormitory for the single men of the hacienda—Mayans, Yaquis, and Chinese—and also for the part-time workers, slaves employed for only half the year, some of whom are married and whose families live in small villages surrounding the hacienda.
Seasonal laborers are found on only about a third of the haciendas and are a class of workers created entirely for the convenience of the masters. They become permanent workers at the masters' discretion and are then allowed to have their families on the hacienda. They are obligated to work more than half the year, if needed, and during the time they are not working on the estate they are not allowed to look for work elsewhere; generally their annual work is divided into two periods: three months in the spring and three in the fall, during which they cannot visit their families. They are kept locked up at night, their food is provided by the estate, and the amount of twelve cents and half a real that they are credited daily is given in small installments to their families so that they do not starve.

From what has been said, it will be seen that the amount credited to a part-time worker in a year, for six months of labor, is $22.50, as total payment, which is what the slave's family relies on to live on for the year.

Time and again I compared, in my imagination, the condition of the slaves in our Southern states before the Civil War, and the Black man always came out on top. Our Southern slaves were almost always well-fed; as a rule, they were not overworked; on many plantations, they were rarely beaten; it was customary to give them some money for small expenses, and they were allowed to leave the plantation at least once a week. These, like the slaves in Yucatán, were livestock belonging to the plantation; but, unlike those, they were treated as well as livestock. In the South before the war, there were not many plantations where more Black people died than were born. The life of our Black slaves was not so harsh, since they could sometimes laugh and sing.
