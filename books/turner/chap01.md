

What is Mexico? North Americans commonly call Mexico "our sister republic."

Most of us vaguely describe it as a republic similar to our own, inhabited by people a little different in temperament, a little poorer, and a little less advanced, but enjoying the protection of republican laws: a free people in the sense that we are free.

Some of us who have seen the country through a train window, or observed it in the mines or on the haciendas, describe this land south of the Rio Grande as governed by a benevolent paternalism, in which a great and good man arranges everything well for his foolish but adored people.

I found that Mexico was none of those things. I discovered that the real Mexico is a country with a Constitution and written laws as generally just and democratic as ours; but where neither the Constitution nor the laws are enforced. Mexico is a country without political freedom, without freedom of speech, without a free press, without free elections, without a judicial system, without political parties, without any of our cherished individual rights, without the freedom to pursue happiness. It is a land where for more than a generation there has been no electoral contest for the Presidency; where the Executive Branch governs everything through a standing army; where political positions are sold at a fixed price. I found that Mexico is a land where people are poor because they have no rights; where peonage is commonplace for the masses and where effective slavery exists for hundreds of thousands of men. Finally, I found that the people do not adore their President: that the mark of the opposition, until now contained and kept at bay by the army and the secret police, will soon breach this wall. Mexicans of all classes and affiliations are in agreement that their country is on the verge of a revolution in favor of democracy: if not a revolution during Díaz's time, since he is already old and expected to die soon, then a revolution after Díaz.

My special interest in Mexican politics was first sparked in early 1908, when I made contact with four Mexican revolutionaries who were then imprisoned in the Los Angeles, California, municipal jail. They were four educated, intelligent, university-educated Mexicans, detained by the United States authorities on charges of planning the invasion of a friendly nation, Mexico, with an armed force from U.S. territory.

Why would cultured men want to take up arms against a republic? Why did they need to come to the United States to prepare their military maneuvers? I spoke with these Mexican detainees. They assured me that for some time they had been peacefully agitating
in their own country to overthrow, without violence and within the constitutional framework, the people
who controlled the government.

But for this very reason, they declared, they had been imprisoned and their property destroyed. The
secret police had been tracking them, their lives threatened, and
every kind of method had been employed to prevent them from continuing their work. Finally, pursued as
criminals beyond national borders, deprived of the rights of freedom of
speech, of the press, and of assembly, deprived of the right to organize peacefully to
promote political change, they had resorted to the only alternative: arms. Why
did they wish to overthrow their government? Because it had disregarded the Constitution; because
it had abolished the civil rights that, according to the consensus of all enlightened men. They are
necessary for the development of a nation: because he had dispossessed the people of their lands:
because he had turned poor workers into serfs, laborers, and some of them even into veritable slaves.
