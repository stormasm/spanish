
CHAPTER VII
THE DÍAZ SYSTEM

### page 53

Slavery and peonage in Mexico, poverty and ignorance, and the general subjugation of the people are, in my humble opinion, due to the financial and political organization that currently governs that country; in a word, to what I will call the "system" of General Porfirio Díaz.

It is true that these conditions have persisted throughout much of Mexico's history, for generations past. I do not wish to be unfair to General Díaz in any way; but although the Spanish made the Mexican people slaves and peons, they never broke or subjected them to the same oppression as they are being broken and destroyed today.

In the time of the Spanish, the peon at least had his small plot of land and his humble hut; but today he has nothing. Furthermore, the Declaration of Independence proclaimed in 1810 also declared the abolition of slavery.

This was abolished, but not entirely: the Mexican governments that followed—class governments, Church governments, or personalist governments— kept the people in servitude, albeit with less severity.

Finally, a democratic movement arose that broke the backbone of the Church; that overthrew the rule of
a caste; that adopted a form of government as modern as the North American one;

The people demonstrated many times that they did not want Díaz as head of government. On three
occasions during the first five years of uprisings, Díaz unsuccessfully ran for
president. In 1867, he obtained just over 30% of the votes that favored
Juárez. In 1871, he launched his candidacy again and lost with roughly three-fifths of the
vote that went to Juárez. In 1872, after Juárez's death, he ran against Lerdo de Tejada and obtained only 1/15 of the votes won by his opponent.

While he was in arms, he was considered just another rebel both at home and abroad; later, he entered the capital of the Republic at the head of a victorious army and proclaimed himself president. Initially, few European nations recognized the upstart's government, and the United States threatened for some time to create complications.

Against the will of the majority of the people, General Díaz assumed leadership of the government 34 years ago; against the will of the majority of the people, he has remained there ever since, except for five years—from 1880 to 1884—when he ceded the National Palace to his close friend Manuel González, with the clear understanding that he would return it at the end of that term.

Since no man can govern a people against their will without depriving them of their freedoms, it is easy to understand what kind of regime General Díaz was forced to establish to secure his power.

Through military force and the police, he controlled elections, the press, and freedom of speech, making popular government a farce. By distributing public offices among his generals, giving them free rein for unrestrained plunder, he ensured his control of the army.

Through political deals with highly esteemed dignitaries in the Catholic Church and by allowing whispers that it would regain its former strength, he won the tacit support of the clergy and the Pope. By promising to pay foreign debts in full and simultaneously launching a campaign to grant concessions and favors to citizens of other countries, especially Americans, he made peace with the rest of the world. In other words, General Díaz, with undeniable skill, seized all the elements of power in the country, except the nation itself.

On the one hand, he exercised a military dictatorship, and on the other, he controlled a financial clique. He himself, the keystone of the arc, was obliged to pay the price for this situation: the price was the entire country. He created a machine whose lubricant was the flesh and blood of the people. He rewarded everyone except the people; this was the sacrifice.

As inevitable as the darkness of night, in contrast to the luminous glory of the dictator, came the degradation of the people: slavery, peonage, and all the miseries that accompany poverty; the abolition of democracy and personal security which fosters foresight, self-respect, and dignified and honorable ambition; In a word, general demoralization, depravity.

Take, for example, Díaz's method of rewarding his military leaders, the men who helped him overthrow the Lerdo government.

As soon as possible after seizing power, he installed his generals as governors in the states and organized them into a national band of exploiters, along with other influential figures in the nation.

In this way, he ensured the continued loyalty of the generals and placed them where he could use them most effectively to keep the people under his control. One form of the rich spoils he distributed among his governors in that early period consisted of private concessions that allowed them to organize companies and build railroads: each concession came with a large sum as a government subsidy.

Thus, the federal government paid for the railroad, and the governor and his most influential friends owned it. Generally, such railroads turned out to be ridiculous, narrow-gauge, and built with the cheapest materials; but the subsidies were very large, enough to lay the tracks and perhaps even equip them. During his first four-year term in power, Díaz issued 71 decrees granting railroad subsidies, representing disbursements of $40 million; all of these decrees, except for those concerning Briseño and Rafael Aguilar, and other properties.

The political chief of Tizimín has notified the town's inhabitants, the owners and workers of the ranches, and other persons located on these lands, that they are obligated to vacate within two months or
be subject to the new owner.

The current occupants have lived on these lands for years, cultivating and improving them
to a great extent. Some have lived there from one generation to the next and have considered themselves the
legal owners, having inherited them from the first "newcomers."

Mr. Rejón García has also denounced other similar national lands in the Espita district.

Another favorite means of confiscating small properties is to arbitrarily assign state taxes. These are levied in Mexico in a threatening and wondrous manner; especially in the less populous districts, landowners are taxed inversely to the degree of sympathy they show toward the person representing the Government in the district in question.

There is no court, board, or other responsible body charged with reviewing unjust contributions. The political chief can impose on a landowner rates five times higher per hectare than those levied on a neighboring landowner, without the former having any way to defend himself, unless he is rich and powerful. He must pay, and if he cannot, the estate will soon be found on the list of properties belonging to the political chief or some of his family members; but if the landowner is rich and powerful, he will most likely not pay any taxes whatsoever. American businessmen in Mexico are tax-exempt almost invariably that the impression has developed in the United States that land in Mexico pays no taxes. Even Frederick Palmer made a statement to that effect in his recent writings about this country.
